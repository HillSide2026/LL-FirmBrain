# Canadian Wood Products — Trade Remedies and Market Access Framework

---

## 1. Purpose

Establish a repeatable, auditable framework for LL projects involving:

- Trade remedies: anti-dumping, countervailing duties, safeguards
- Market access strategy
- Regulatory and tariff navigation

Specifically for Canadian wood products, including lumber, engineered wood, and
pulp inputs where relevant.

---

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|

| Traceability required | All LL outputs must be traceable to approved doctrine or clearly labeled draft analysis |
| No policy invention | Where gaps exist, flag to ML1 — do not fill |

## 3. Core Workstreams

### A. Trade Remedies

**Objective:** Determine exposure and positioning in foreign jurisdictions.

**Standard steps:**

1. Identify applicable regime:
   - Anti-dumping
   - Countervailing duties (CVD)
   - Safeguards

2. Map governing authorities:

   | Jurisdiction | Authority |
   |---|---|
   | Canada — investigation | Canada Border Services Agency (CBSA) |
   | Canada — injury | Canadian International Trade Tribunal (CITT) |
   | United States — investigation | U.S. Department of Commerce (DOC) |
   | United States — injury | U.S. International Trade Commission (USITC) |
   | Multilateral | World Trade Organization (WTO) |

3. Identify procedural posture:
   - Investigation stage (preliminary or final)
   - Review (expiry review, interim review, changed circumstances)
   - Appeal (Federal Court of Appeal, CITT, WTO panel)

**Required outputs:**

- Exposure memo (jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction)
- Duty risk range (where applicable)
- Procedural posture summary (investigation stage, review, appeal)

---

### B. Market Access Strategy

**Objective:** Identify potential scope of or channels for entry and expansion
while minimizing duty and compliance friction.

**Standard components:**

1. Tariff classification validation (HS code verification)
2. Origin analysis (USMCA rules of origin where applicable)
3. Non-tariff barrier identification:
   - Phytosanitary rules
   - Environmental certifications
   - Local procurement biases or domestic content requirements

**Key frameworks:**

- USMCA (Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement)
- WTO Agreement on Subsidies and Countervailing Measures (SCM Agreement)
- WTO Anti-Dumping Agreement

**Required outputs:**

- Market entry pathway memo
- Barrier mitigation strategy
- Structuring recommendations (labeled Draft for ML1 Approval until ML1 review)

### C. Canadian Wood Products Focus Layer

This layer applies to every file in this practice area. It is not optional.

**Critical context — include in every file:**

| Context item | Substance |
|---|---|
| Softwood lumber dispute history | Canada-U.S. softwood lumber dispute is the dominant backdrop; provincial stumpage systems are the persistent subsidy allegation; the current SLA framework (or absence of one) governs the live duty exposure |
| Provincial stumpage systems | Provincial pricing of Crown timber is routinely alleged by U.S. petitioners as a countervailable subsidy; the characterization varies by province and species |
| Species and product differentiation | Different products carry different duty exposure and classification risk; do not aggregate |

**Product map:**

| Product category | Examples | Key risk |
|---|---|---|
| Softwood lumber | SPF, Douglas fir, hemlock | Core softwood lumber dispute; AD/CVD orders in U.S. |
| Engineered wood | Cross-laminated timber (CLT), laminated veneer lumber (LVL) | Classification risk — may fall inside or outside lumber orders depending on HS code and scope determination |
| Panels | OSB, plywood, MDF | Separate duty exposure from lumber; plywood from China currently subject to active PI-2026-001 at CITT |
| Pulp inputs | Where relevant | Separate regulatory regime; flag to ML1 if pulp inputs are in scope |

**Baseline risk flags — assess in every file:**

- Subsidy characterization risk via provincial stumpage pricing
- Dumping margin methodology: benchmark selection and comparison market assumptions drive margin outcomes
- U.S. political sensitivity: softwood lumber is directly linked to U.S. housing affordability; political exposure elevates enforcement risk above pure legal analysis

### D. Target Sub-Segments

Sub-sectors where LL is actively pursuing files or where opportunity density
is highest under current market and regulatory conditions.

| Sub-segment | Indicative HS range | Primary opportunity type | Current status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plywood and wood-based panels | 4412.xx | Import duty exposure; representation in anti-dumping / CVD proceedings; importer compliance | TR-003 active; PI-2026-001 in progress |
| Processed and secondary wood goods | 4409.xx, 4418.xx | Safeguard exposure; import and export positioning; proceeding participation | TR-004 active; GC-2026-001 in progress |
| Softwood lumber (SPF, Douglas fir, hemlock) | 4407.xx, 4409.xx | Canada-U.S. AD/CVD compliance; expiry review participation; market access diversification away from U.S. dependency | Background risk layer on all files; no active file |
| Engineered wood (CLT, LVL, glulam) | 4412.xx, 4413.xx | Classification risk management; market access advisory; potential future duty scope rulings | Known gap — no standardized LL doctrine yet |

HS codes are indicative only. Tariff classification must be validated per file.
Do not apply a sub-segment's HS range to a client without a classification
analysis.

### E. Sensitive Sub-Segments

Sub-sectors carrying elevated political, regulatory, or commercial sensitivity
that require heightened care on every file. Sensitivity does not mean avoid;
it means flag, scope carefully, and confirm ML1 awareness before proceeding.

| Sub-segment | Sensitivity level | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Softwood lumber (SPF, Douglas fir, hemlock) | Maximum | Decades-long Canada-U.S. bilateral dispute; direct linkage to U.S. housing affordability and political cycle; provincial stumpage systems are the persistent CVD flashpoint; any file touching SPF or Douglas fir exporters risks being drawn into the broader dispute regardless of the specific legal question |
| Plywood (China-origin, Canadian importers) | High | PI-2026-001 pits domestic Canadian producers (complainants seeking protection) against Canadian importers (opposing duties); representing either side requires a conflict check and explicit scope control; the same Andersen contact must not be on both sides |
| Mass timber — CLT and LVL | Medium-high | U.S. lumber industry has sought to extend existing lumber order scope to cover CLT and LVL; classification outcome is unsettled; Canadian federal and provincial governments are promoting mass timber as an embodied-carbon climate solution, creating political tension with enforcement; files touching CLT or LVL must flag the classification risk explicitly |
| Wood pellets | Medium | Intersection of trade law and environmental regulation; EU CBAM and biomass sustainability certification requirements create non-tariff barrier exposure in European export markets; not core AD/CVD territory but regulatory risk in market access files |
| Engineered wood inputs used in U.S. residential construction | Medium | U.S. housing affordability politics extend beyond lumber to any wood input used in homebuilding; elevated enforcement sensitivity and congressional attention; files with a U.S. residential construction nexus should be flagged to ML1 regardless of the specific product |

## 4. Andersen Consulting Interface Protocol

**Purpose:** Maintain a clean boundary between LL legal analysis and Andersen
Consulting advisory outputs.

### Permitted

- Translating ML1-approved legal positions into structured inputs for Andersen
- Receiving market and operational data from Andersen for use in legal analysis

### Not Permitted

- Delegating legal reasoning to Andersen
- Accepting Andersen consultant conclusions as legal positions without ML1
  review

### Labeling rule

All materials received from Andersen Consulting must be labeled:

> **Third-party input — not validated**

This label must appear on the document or in the file note recording receipt of
the material. It does not disappear until ML1 explicitly adopts the content as
an approved LL position.

## 7. Known Gaps

The following areas have no standardized LL doctrine as of the date of this
document. Analysis in these areas must be labeled **Draft for ML1 Approval**
and flagged explicitly until doctrine is established.

| Gap | Notes |
|---|---|
| Engineered wood vs. lumber differential treatment | CLT and LVL may fall inside or outside existing lumber orders depending on scope rulings; no standardized LL position |
| Carbon border adjustments | Emerging regimes (EU CBAM, potential Canadian equivalent) are not yet captured in this framework; flag any file that touches carbon pricing at import as a gap area |
