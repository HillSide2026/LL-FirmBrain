Three Core Distinctions

Three distinctions underpin virtually every payment system — and shape how risk is allocated in a real-time environment.

First

Messaging vs. Funds Movement

Second

Clearing vs. Settlement

Third

Direct vs. Indirect Participation

2.1
2.1 — First Distinction

Messaging vs. Funds Movement

A payment message is an instruction that identifies who is paying, who is receiving, the amount, and the routing information. The message itself is not money.

Historically, systems could transmit payment instructions more quickly than they could clear and settle the underlying obligation.

This raises a critical threshold question:

"What exactly is happening in real time?"

Option A

Real-time messaging only

Option B

Real-time clearing

Option C

Real-time settlement

RTR Objective

All three — real-time clearing and settlement of payment instructions, not just message transmission

2.1
2.1 — Second Distinction

Clearing vs. Settlement

Clearing

  • The process of identifying, validating, reconciling, and calculating payment obligations between participants
  • Determines what is owed — to whom, and by how much
  • Does not itself transfer value

Settlement

  • The discharge of those obligations — the transfer of value
  • The point at which payment obligations are satisfied
  • Settlement is final and, in most systems, irrevocable
RTR Implication

Historically, clearing and settlement were separated by hours or days — providing time for error identification, liquidity positioning, and exception handling. RTR compresses this interval. Institutions have less time to manage liquidity, intervene when issues arise, or prevent problematic transactions from completing.

2.1
2.1 — Third Distinction

Direct vs. Indirect Participation

Not all participants interact with payment infrastructure in the same way. A participant's position within the payment chain determines who holds each responsibility.

Who controls the payment message?
Who bears liquidity obligations?
Who performs sanctions screening?
Who manages operational incidents?
Who assumes settlement risk?
Who bears responsibility when a payment fails?
Key Insight

Access to a payment system and responsibility within a payment system are not necessarily the same thing. The question is not simply whether an organization participates in RTR — but how it participates, and which risks and responsibilities accompany that role.

2.1
2.2 — Overview

Why These Distinctions Matter

These distinctions matter for at least three interconnected reasons. Each shapes how institutions must redesign their operations for a real-time environment.

01

Liquidity

Funds must be available at the point of payment — not at end of day. Real-time systems demand continuous liquidity management.

02

Controls

Compliance, fraud, and error risks must increasingly be addressed before a payment is sent — not after. The window to intervene is gone.

03

Responsibility

When something goes wrong, unwinding the transaction is limited. The allocation of risk becomes sharp-edged.

2.2
2.2.1 — Liquidity Management

Managing Liquidity in Real Time

Traditional / Batch

Payment obligations accumulate, are netted or reconciled, and settle on predetermined schedules.

Funding decisions are made with reference to known settlement windows. Liquidity managed over the course of a business day.

Real-Time (RTR) — 24 / 7 / 365

Funds must generally be available at or near the time the payment is processed — at any hour, any day.

No settlement window. Obligations arise, are processed, and become final within seconds.

Participants must continuously determine:

How much liquidity to maintain

Where liquidity should be positioned

How liquidity will be replenished

How liquidity will be monitored continuously

How to manage liquidity outside traditional business hours

Failure to manage = transaction limits, queue formation, payment delays

2.2.1
2.2.2 — Controls

Rethinking the Control Framework

Legacy / Batch Environment

Transactions can be reviewed before settlement occurs. Unusual activity can be investigated. Exceptions can be escalated. Fraud alerts can be assessed. Time functions as a risk-mitigation tool.

→

Real-Time Environment

Payments initiated, processed, cleared, and settled within seconds. Institutions have less time to recognize problems, intervene, and prevent losses before a transaction becomes final.

The Shift

From corrective controls (identifying problems after initiation) to preventative controls (preventing problematic transactions before they are initiated). This shift is operational, compliance-related, and organizational — not merely technological.

2.2.2
2.2.2.1 — Fraud Controls

Fraud in a Real-Time Environment

In a batch environment, a suspicious transaction may be identified and intercepted before settlement. In a real-time environment, the same transaction may be cleared and settled before any meaningful manual review can occur. Institutions must increasingly rely on preventative, automated controls applied before payment instructions are released.

Three Practical Consequences

01

Friction vs. Speed

How much friction is appropriate? Customers expect immediacy. But additional verification may be necessary to manage fraud risk. Balancing experience and security becomes a design decision.

02

Automation & Accountability

How much authority to delegate to automated systems? Fraud models operate at speed humans cannot match — but greater automation increases false positive risk and accountability gaps.

03

Loss Allocation

How are fraud losses allocated when transactions are increasingly difficult to reverse? Recovery options narrow as settlement accelerates. Prevention becomes the primary line of defence.

Key Takeaway

Real-time payments do not eliminate fraud risk. They change the point at which and the ways in which fraud risk must be managed.

2.2.2.1
2.2.2.2 — Financial Crime Controls

AML, Sanctions & ATF in Real Time

The Principle

Real-time payments do not reduce AML, ATF, or sanctions compliance obligations. They reduce the amount of time available to satisfy them — creating pressure to shift from detective to preventative compliance controls.

Four Governance Challenges

Challenge 1 — Automation vs. Human Review

Which compliance decisions can be automated? Which require human oversight? Real-time environments reduce opportunities to investigate before settlement, creating pressure to automate while maintaining governance.

Challenge 2 — Escalation Procedures

Many compliance frameworks assume hours or days for review. Real-time systems require decision-making frameworks capable of operating within seconds — including sanctions screening.

Challenge 3 — Customer Expectations

Situations will arise where speed, convenience, and risk management are not perfectly aligned. Compliance frameworks must account for this tension.

Challenge 4 — 24 / 7 Operational Resilience

Sanctions controls, fraud monitoring, alert management, and escalation mechanisms must function continuously — outside traditional business hours. A 9–5 framework is not sufficient.

Key Takeaway

Institutions that adapt most successfully will redesign their compliance frameworks to operate effectively within a real-time environment — rather than applying batch-era controls to real-time payment systems.

2.2.2.2
2.2.3 — Responsibility

How Responsibility Is Allocated

A single payment transaction may involve a customer, a financial institution, a payment service provider, a technology platform, a sponsor institution, a settlement participant, and the system operator. Responsibility is distributed — and indirect participation does not transfer it.

Questions That Must Be Answered

Who is responsible for sanctions screening?
Who is responsible for AML compliance?
Who is responsible for customer authentication?
Who bears losses arising from fraud?
Who manages operational incidents?
Who bears liability for failed or delayed payments?
How Answers Are Determined

Legislation, payment-system rules, participation agreements, contractual arrangements, and operational practices — together, these allocate responsibility among participants.

The Core Principle

Real-time payment systems do not eliminate risk. They redistribute it. Organizations must understand the statutory, operational, and contractual frameworks that allocate responsibility before joining the system.
