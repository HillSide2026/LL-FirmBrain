Section 3

Operational Risks, Fraud & Outage Mitigation

Real-time payment systems produce a second and equally important consequence beyond compressed mitigation windows: as payments become faster and continuously available, operational resilience becomes a core component of payment system design.

Before (Batch)

A system outage results in a delayed batch file — invisible to most customers until the next settlement window.

After (Real-Time)

A system outage prevents customers from sending or receiving funds right now — immediately visible to customers, counterparties, and regulators.

The Result

Not necessarily more risk — but a different risk profile. Real-time payments transform operational resilience from a back-office issue into a customer-facing issue.

3
3.1

Operational Risks

Two categories of operational risk become particularly significant within real-time payment environments.

3.1.1

System Outages

Immediate customer impact, reputational consequences, and liquidity disruption when availability expectations are not met.

3.1.2

Dependency & Concentration Risk

Operational disruptions arising through the failure of third-party providers — even when an institution's own systems are functioning.

3.1
3.1.1 — System Outage

Not All Outages Are the Same

Because payments are expected to be available continuously, outages now have immediate consequences for customers, counterparties, and market participants. A payment system may experience:

Initiation Failure

Customers unable to submit payment instructions

Processing Failure

Payment messages cannot be validated or routed

Settlement Failure

Payment obligations cannot be completed

Connectivity Failure

One institution loses access to the payment rail

Infrastructure Failure

Broader disruption affecting multiple participants simultaneously

Expectation Gap

For customers, the distinction between outage types is irrelevant. The experience is simply that a payment expected to be immediate is unavailable. Even short disruptions generate reputational consequences, escalations, and regulatory scrutiny.

Outage Management Requirements

System redundancy Business continuity planning Incident management procedures Vendor resilience Communications protocols

The question is less whether an outage will occur — and more how quickly it can be detected, contained, communicated, and resolved.

3.1.2 — Dependency & Concentration Risk

When Third Parties Fail

Dependency Risk

Arises when an institution's ability to provide payment services depends on the continued availability of systems operated by another party.

Sponsor institution

Payment rail access

Cloud service provider

Infrastructure

Sanctions screening vendor

Compliance controls

Fraud-monitoring platform

Transaction analysis

Identity verification vendor

Customer onboarding

Concentration Risk

Arises when multiple institutions rely upon the same third parties — so that a single provider failure cascades across a large portion of the payment ecosystem.

Cascade Effect

A single cloud provider or payment processor failure may simultaneously disrupt many participants — creating systemic rather than isolated exposure.

Institutions must understand not only their own technology environments, but also the operational dependencies that support their payment activities throughout the ecosystem.

Operational Risk Management Requires

Vendor due diligence  ·  Contractual protections  ·  Service-level monitoring  ·  Incident reporting obligations  ·  Business continuity planning  ·  Contingency arrangements

3.2 — Regulatory Relevance

Operational Risk as a Regulatory Expectation: The RPAA

Operational Concern Only?

Historically, operational risk within payment systems was primarily a commercial and reputational concern, addressed through internal policies and service agreements.

→

Increasingly, Both

As payment activity increasingly involves technology companies, payment processors, and non-bank participants, regulators have focused on operational resilience as a matter of policy and law.

RPAA Emphasis Areas

Pillar 1

Operational risk management — frameworks for identifying and mitigating operational disruption

Pillar 2

Incident response — detection, containment, and reporting of operational incidents

Pillar 3

Safeguarding end-user funds — ensuring customer funds are protected through disruption

Pillar 4

Reporting obligations — notification requirements to regulators and affected parties

Key Takeaway

For organizations participating in modern payment ecosystems, operational resilience is no longer merely a commercial consideration. It has become a regulatory expectation.

Institutions evaluating RTR participation must consider both payment system rules and the regulatory framework governing operational risk.

3.2
3.3 — Operational Controls

Controls as Substitutes for Mitigation Windows

What changes in real-time environments is not necessarily the nature of controls, but the speed, consistency, and reliability with which they must operate. Operational controls increasingly serve as substitutes for the mitigation windows that batch systems previously provided.

Velocity Limits

Restrict the number or value of transactions within a defined period — per customer, account, device, or channel. Act as an early-warning mechanism for abnormal behaviour.

Transaction Tiering

Apply different control frameworks based on transaction value, customer profile, and risk rating — allocating scrutiny proportionately rather than uniformly.

Circuit Breakers

Interrupt or slow payment activity when predefined thresholds are exceeded. Recreate the intervention window that batch systems historically provided.

Fallback Rails

Alternative payment channels or contingency processes that preserve critical payment functionality during periods of operational disruption.

3.3
3.3 — Operational Control

Velocity Limits

Velocity controls restrict the number or value of transactions within a specified period. They may be applied per customer, per account, per device, per counterparty, or per payment channel.

What Unusual Velocity May Signal

  • Account compromise or takeover
  • Automated or scripted attacks
  • System malfunction or operational error
  • Attempts to circumvent established controls

Why Effective in Real-Time Environments

  • Operate automatically — no manual intervention required
  • Trigger before losses or disruptions manifest
  • Apply across fraud prevention, compliance, and operational objectives
  • Preserve speed and efficiency while creating scrutiny thresholds
Key Insight

Velocity controls are frequently associated with fraud prevention — but they also serve broader operational objectives. By establishing predefined thresholds, institutions create mechanisms to identify abnormal behaviour before losses or operational disruptions manifest. In many respects, velocity controls function as an early-warning mechanism within real-time payment systems.

3.3
3.3 — Operational Control

Transaction Tiering

Not all transactions present the same level of risk. Transaction tiering applies different control frameworks based on factors including transaction value, customer profile, historical activity, geographic exposure, product type, and overall risk rating.

Lower Risk

Processed automatically — no additional friction applied

Medium Risk

Enhanced monitoring triggered — automated flags raised for review

Higher Risk

Additional authentication, escalation, or manual review required

The Balance

Effective tiering frameworks seek to balance speed, customer convenience, and risk management by applying additional monitoring only where circumstances warrant. Excessive controls undermine the customer experience; insufficient controls compromise fraud and compliance risk management. Tiering allocates resources proportionately rather than uniformly.

3.3
3.3 — Operational Control

Circuit Breakers

Circuit breakers are designed to interrupt, slow, or restrict payment activity when predefined thresholds are exceeded — creating an opportunity for intervention before a problem escalates.

Triggering Conditions

  • Unusual or elevated transaction volumes
  • Elevated fraud indicators or alert rates
  • Liquidity shortages or funding gaps
  • System performance degradation
  • Abnormal payment patterns suggesting broader systems issues

Possible Responses

  • Additional authentication requirements
  • Transaction value or volume limits
  • Temporary payment restrictions on specific channels
  • Manual review procedures for flagged transactions
  • Suspension of specific payment functionality
Key Insight

In many respects, circuit breakers recreate the mitigation window that batch systems historically provided — giving institutions a structured mechanism to slow down payment activity when the surrounding control environment indicates elevated risk. The objective is not to stop normal activity, but to create space for intervention before a problem escalates.

3.3
3.3 — Operational Control

Fallback Rails

No payment system is infallible. A fallback rail is an alternative payment channel or contingency process that may be used when a primary payment system becomes unavailable or experiences significant operational degradation.

Fallback Options May Include

  • Traditional EFT / batch-payment processing
  • Wire transfer arrangements
  • Alternative payment processors
  • Secondary connectivity solutions
  • Manual contingency procedures

Effective Fallback Planning Requires

Identify critical payment functions in advance

Understand which services must be maintained during disruption

Establish clear escalation procedures

Periodically test contingency arrangements

Key Insight

The objective is not to replicate the full functionality of the primary payment rail. It is to preserve the institution's ability to perform critical payment activities during disruption. Fallback planning should be treated as an ongoing component of operational resilience — regularly reviewed, tested, and updated — not a static business continuity exercise.

Section 3 — Observation

Proactivity, Governance & Culture

Real-time payment systems require institutions to identify, assess, and respond to risk not only more quickly, but also more proactively.

01

Embedded Controls

Operational controls designed into the payment process — not layered on top of it after the fact.

02

Agile Governance

Governance frameworks capable of responding within the compressed timelines that real-time systems demand.

03

Risk Discipline

A culture where risk management is embedded in day-to-day operations — not reserved for exceptional events.
