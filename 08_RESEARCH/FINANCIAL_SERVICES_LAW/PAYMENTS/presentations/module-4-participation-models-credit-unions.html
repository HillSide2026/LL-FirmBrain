Section 4

Participation Models for Credit Unions

Throughout this presentation we have examined liquidity management, fraud controls, operational resilience, and regulatory expectations. The practical question is how those considerations influence participation decisions.

Each institution's participation model will typically determine risk, cost, control, and operational obligations more than any other single factor.

The Question

Not merely whether to participate in a real-time payment ecosystem — but how.

This discussion is framed from the perspective of a credit union. Before examining the available participation models, it is useful to consider the strategic stakes — with particular focus on Ontario.

4
4.1 — Business Case

The Credit Union Business Case

Potential Opportunities

  • Retain members who expect bank-equivalent payment functionality
  • Improve business banking offerings
  • Faster account funding and treasury services
  • Better integration with digital banking and commercial cash-management tools
  • Reduce dependence on slower payment rails for certain use cases
  • Participate in Canada's evolving real-time payment ecosystem

The Challenge

The challenge is generally not identifying a use case. The challenge is justifying the investment, liquidity management requirements, operational resilience obligations, and continuous-service expectations that accompany participation.

The Premise

Can the institution support the liquidity, operational, compliance, and resilience obligations that participation entails — and on what timeline? Once that question is accepted, the focus shifts away from whether participation is desirable toward how it can be supported safely, sustainably, and economically.

4.1
4.2 — Strategic Questions

Who Pays For It?

Real-time payment participation involves significant investment across multiple functions — technology, operations, treasury, fraud management, cybersecurity, compliance, and customer support. The economics of that investment are not the same for every institution.

Large Financial Institutions

Can spread technology investments, operational teams, treasury functions, fraud-management capabilities, cybersecurity resources, compliance programs, and customer-support functions across millions of customers.

Fixed costs of participation are absorbed across a large transaction base — improving per-unit economics at scale.

Smaller Institutions

Often cannot spread those same costs across an equivalent customer base. The question is whether the benefits of member retention and business banking growth justify the operational cost.

In many cases, this is less a technology question than a scale question. The economics of participation often improve materially as transaction volumes increase.

Insight

The question is not whether participation would be beneficial — it often would. The question is whether the institution can build the operational and financial model to support it sustainably.

4.2
4.2 — Strategic Questions

How Much Liquidity Must Be Committed?

Historically

Liquidity management was primarily a treasury function. Payment obligations accumulated, were netted, and settled on predetermined schedules. Funding decisions were made with reference to known settlement windows.

→

Real-Time Systems

Liquidity management becomes a daily operational requirement. Funds must be available at or near the moment the payment is processed — 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year.

Institutions Must Determine

How much liquidity to maintain at all times

What buffers are required for elevated volume periods

How liquidity will be replenished continuously

How unexpected payment volumes will be managed

How liquidity will be monitored and managed outside traditional business hours

Governance Implication

These are not merely treasury questions. They are questions about capital allocation, operating model design, and the economics of participation.

4.2
4.2 — Strategic Questions

Can We Operate 24 / 7?

Many financial institutions remain operationally organized around business hours. Real-time payment systems operate continuously — and require the operational infrastructure to match.

Continuous Monitoring

Transaction monitoring, fraud alerts, and system health checks that operate outside business hours

Continuous Incident Response

On-call procedures, escalation paths, and resolution capabilities available at any hour

Continuous Fraud Management

Automated and human fraud oversight that does not pause at 5:00 p.m.

Continuous Vendor Oversight

Monitoring of critical service providers whose failures may become immediately visible to customers

Continuous Availability

Customer expectation that payment services will be accessible at all times — with no planned downtime window

The Shift

Organizations that historically operated on banking schedules must begin thinking more like operators of critical infrastructure

Key Question

For some institutions, 24/7 operational readiness may be the most significant change required — more significant than the technology investment itself.

4.2
4.2 — Strategic Questions

Can We Manage Real-Time Fraud?

Historically, time itself functioned as a control. Mitigation windows between initiation, clearing, and settlement provided time to detect, investigate, and intervene.

Real-time payment systems significantly reduce or eliminate those windows. Institutions must increasingly rely on preventative, automated controls applied before a payment instruction is transmitted.

Required Capabilities

Automated fraud detection Behavioural analytics Transaction monitoring Device intelligence Authentication controls Real-time risk scoring Velocity controls Circuit breakers
Investment Scope

The investment required frequently extends beyond payment technology itself — into fraud operations, staffing, governance, and vendor relationships. These are operational decisions, not merely technology decisions.

The Shift

From corrective controls applied after initiation → to preventative controls applied before initiation. The control framework must be designed for the speed of the payment system, not built for a batch environment.

Can We Meet Resilience Expectations?

Real-time payment systems create new uptime and availability expectations. An outage at 11:00 p.m. may be as significant as one at 11:00 a.m. Institutions must have answers across every dimension of resilience.

System redundancy Cyber resilience Disaster recovery Dependency risk management Concentration risk management Business continuity planning Incident response capabilities

What operating model must we build to support real-time payments safely and sustainably?

What Comes Next

These strategic questions converge on a single governance decision: the choice of participation model. The following sections examine two principal models — direct participation and sponsored participation — and the trade-offs each entails.

4.2
4.3 — Participation Models

Direct Participation

Under direct participation, an institution connects directly to the payment rail and performs the functions required of participants under the applicable payment-system framework.

Potential Advantages

  • Greater operational control over payment functions
  • Direct access to payment infrastructure and functionality
  • Greater product flexibility and faster iteration
  • Enhanced strategic independence
  • Reduced reliance on intermediaries
  • Direct visibility into payment operations and data

Responsibilities Assumed

  • Connectivity infrastructure and technical integration
  • Liquidity management — continuous, 24/7
  • Operational resilience programs and uptime obligations
  • Fraud-management controls
  • Compliance, sanctions screening, and AML obligations
  • Business continuity and disaster recovery procedures
  • Ongoing governance and rule-compliance participation
Trade-Off

Direct participation maximizes control, but it also concentrates responsibility. For larger institutions with sufficient scale and operational maturity, that trade-off may be attractive. For smaller institutions, the operational burden may outweigh the benefits.

4.3
4.4 — Participation Models

Sponsored Participation

A sponsored or indirect participation model allows an institution to access payment infrastructure through another participant. Common throughout global payment systems — often the most practical path for smaller institutions.

Potential Advantages

  • Lower implementation costs and technology investment
  • Faster speed to market
  • Reduced operational complexity
  • Reduced connectivity requirements
  • Access to payment functionality through an established participant
  • Often the most practical path for smaller credit unions

Key Risks & Dependencies

  • Operational resilience of the sponsor
  • Sponsor's technology infrastructure and uptime
  • Sponsor's compliance and sanctions framework
  • Sponsor's liquidity management practices
  • Sponsor's strategic priorities and business continuity
  • Allocation of responsibility for fraud, AML, authentication, and failed payments
Trade-Off

Sponsored participation reduces operational burden, but increases dependency. The key governance question shifts from "How do we operate the payment rail?" to "How do we oversee the parties that operate it on our behalf?"

4.4
4.5 — Choosing the Right Model

There Is No Universally Right Model

The best model depends on the institution's size, transaction volume, technological capabilities, risk appetite, available resources, and strategic objectives. Institutions should evaluate each of the following dimensions.

Control

How much control over payment operations, customer experience, product development, and strategic direction does the institution wish to retain?

Cost

What level of investment in infrastructure, staffing, liquidity management, compliance, and operational resilience is the institution prepared to make?

Liquidity

Who manages liquidity requirements? How much must be committed? How will it be monitored and replenished continuously?

Compliance

Who performs CDD, sanctions screening, transaction monitoring, fraud management, and regulatory reporting?

Operational Resilience

Who manages outages, responds to incidents, and owns business continuity? What happens if a critical service provider becomes unavailable?

Governance

How are responsibilities allocated? How are disputes resolved? How are risks allocated contractually between participants?

4.5
Conclusions

Participation Decisions Are Governance Decisions

01

No Model Eliminates Risk

Each participation model allocates risk differently — among the institution, the sponsor, the system operator, and the customer.

02

Understand Before You Implement

The most successful participants understand the operational, compliance, liquidity, and governance consequences of their chosen model before implementation begins.

03

Sustainability Over Participation

For credit unions, the objective is not simply to participate — it is to identify a model and use cases that are sustainable and resilient while consistent with long-term strategic objectives.

The Core Principle

Participation decisions determine how an institution assumes responsibility for liquidity management, operational resilience, compliance, fraud controls, and governance. These are not technology decisions. They are decisions about who an institution wants to be — and what obligations it is prepared to own.
