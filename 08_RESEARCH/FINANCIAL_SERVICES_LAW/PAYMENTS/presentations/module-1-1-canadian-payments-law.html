The Law

Canada's payments framework rests on two federal statutes — supported by an operational layer of rulebooks, participation agreements, settlement arrangements, and technical standards.

1.1.1
1.1.1 — The Law

The Framework

The statutes create the framework. The operational layer is built on four supporting pillars.

Rulebooks

Binding rules governing system operation and participant conduct — promulgated by Payments Canada and RTR governance bodies.

Participation Agreements

Contractual instruments executed by each participant on joining a payment system. They operationalize the rulebook at the participant level.

Settlement Arrangements

Agreements governing how funds are moved and settled — including Bank of Canada settlement accounts and funding commitments.

Technical Standards

ISO 20022, certification requirements, connectivity protocols, and fraud services manuals that govern system integration.

1.1.1
1.1.1 — The Law

The Governing Statutes

Canadian Payments Act

  • Establishes Payments Canada as the national payment association
  • Governs the operation of Canada's payment clearing and settlement systems
  • Sets the mandate, membership structure, and governance of Payments Canada
Role

Governs the operator of the rails

Payment Clearing and Settlement Act (PCSA)

  • Grants the Bank of Canada oversight authority over payment and settlement systems
  • Applies to systems designated as systemically important
  • Authorizes the Bank to issue directives on system design and risk controls
Role

Sets governance expectations for the safety and stability of the rails

1.1.1
In a Nutshell

Two statutes.
Two distinct roles.

Canadian Payments Act

Governs an operator for the rails — Payments Canada, the institution responsible for Canada's clearing and settlement infrastructure.

Payment Clearing and Settlement Act

Sets governance expectations for the safety and stability of the rails — Bank of Canada oversight over systemically important payment systems.

1.1.2

Payment Systems

Canada's clearing and settlement infrastructure spans three primary systems — each designed for a different tier of payment activity.

1.1.2
1.1.2 — Payment Systems

Lynx — High-Value Settlement

Lynx
Canada's high-value payment system, operated by Payments Canada.
Settlement Model

Real-Time Gross Settlement (RTGS) — each transaction settled individually and immediately, in real time.

Target Use

Large-value and time-critical payments: interbank transfers, securities settlement, large commercial flows.

Not Designed For

Consumer retail payments. Lynx is not a retail payment rail — it is a high-value wholesale system.

The Gap

Lynx provides real-time settlement for large transactions. Canada does not yet have a broadly accessible, real-time retail payment rail.

1.1.2
1.1.2 — Payment Systems

ACSS — Retail Batch Clearing

Automated Clearing Settlement System (ACSS)
Canada's principal retail batch clearing system.
Processing Model

Batch-based — transactions are accumulated and cleared in cycles, not individually or in real time.

Settlement Timing

Deferred settlement — finality occurs after the clearing cycle, not at the moment of the payment instruction.

Common Uses

Pre-authorized debits, direct deposits, cheque clearing, and routine electronic funds transfers.

Market Position

Despite the emergence of newer systems, many businesses continue to rely heavily on ACSS rails for their payment flows.

1.1.2
1.1.2 — Payment Systems

Interac e-Transfer

Interac e-Transfer
Canada's closest equivalent to a retail real-time payment rail — operated by Interac Corp.
User Experience

Near-instant payment experience for end users — funds appear to arrive immediately at the recipient's account.

Access Model

Available exclusively through participating financial institutions — not a directly accessible open network for non-bank players.

Technical Reality

Interac e-Transfer is not a true open-access Real-Time Payment (RTP) system from a technical or regulatory architecture perspective.

Implication for Fintechs

Interac fills the retail real-time gap today, but its closed-access structure limits direct participation by credit unions and fintechs.

1.1.3
1.1.3 — Key Takeaway

Where Obligations Actually Live

Most operational obligations governing RTR participation are not found in legislation. They live in:

  • RTR By-laws and RTR Rulebook — the primary governance layer setting rights, obligations, and risk allocation for real-time rail participants
  • Participant-specific participation agreements — obligations tailored to each participant's role and access tier
  • Settlement account, funding, and liquidity arrangements — the financial obligations that underpin settlement finality
  • Technical standards — ISO 20022, certification, connectivity, and fraud services requirements
Practical Significance

This is where credit unions and fintechs will ultimately look for the practical rights, obligations, and risk allocations governing their RTR participation.
